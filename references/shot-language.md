# Shot Language Reference

Use this reference when designing a storyboard table, shot list, or generation prompt.

## Panel Anatomy

A useful storyboard panel contains the viewer frame, the active story beat, the subject action, camera shot, camera movement, dialogue or vocal note when relevant, and short technical annotations. Camera arrows and written camera notes should work together so the intended movement is unambiguous.

## Shot Size

1. Extreme wide shot
   Use for scale, isolation, geography, huge architecture, weather, crowds, or a tiny subject overwhelmed by the world.

2. Wide shot
   Use for full body action, choreography, combat, running, dance, jumping, or spatial relationships.

3. Full shot
   Use when limbs, props, floor contact, and silhouette must stay readable.

4. Medium shot
   Use for body language, torso action, social tension, and performance beats.

5. Medium close up
   Use for emotional pressure while retaining shoulders, hands, or microphone movement.

6. Close up
   Use for face, breath, eye direction, hand tremor, impact reaction, or decisive prop contact.

7. Extreme close up
   Use for a flash of detail: eye, mouth, rope bite, sneaker sole, finger, sweat, switch, reflection, lyric emphasis.

8. Insert
   Use for an object that controls the beat: key, blade, phone, rope, pedal, drum pad, match, blood drop.

9. Point of view
   Use when the audience should inhabit a character, target, obstacle, pursuer, or falling body.

10. Over the shoulder
   Use for confrontation, watching, discovery, or a character entering another person perspective.

## Camera Angle

1. Eye level
   Neutral, direct, human scale.

2. Low angle
   Power, heroism, menace, monumentality, impossible height.

3. High angle
   Vulnerability, overview, exposure, isolation, surveillance.

4. Overhead
   Geometry, ritual, choreography maps, floor patterns, strategic clarity.

5. Birdseye
   World as diagram, fate, trap, crowd pattern, maze.

6. Wormseye
   Extreme upward force, vertigo, dominance, falling debris, heroic jump.

7. Dutch angle
   Instability, impact, panic, intoxication, broken reality.

8. Profile
   Graphic silhouette, speed, chase, dance line, emotional distance.

9. Three quarter
   Body depth, readable face plus movement, good default for action staging.

10. Reverse angle
   Reaction, reveal, cause and effect, spatial reset.

## Camera Movement

1. Static
   Use when the subject or environment already carries enough motion, or when a detail must be read clearly.

2. Pan
   Camera turns horizontally. Use to follow lateral movement, reveal a space, or connect two subjects.

3. Tilt
   Camera turns vertically. Use to reveal height, falling, rising, power shifts, or a body moving up and down.

4. Whip pan
   Fast smear between beats. Use for shock, rhythm, impact, speed, or music synced cuts.

5. Dolly in or push in
   Camera moves toward subject. Use to intensify attention, threat, revelation, or emotional commitment.

6. Dolly out or pull back
   Camera retreats. Use to reveal scale, loneliness, aftermath, trap, or collapse of certainty.

7. Tracking
   Camera travels with the subject. Use for chase, dance, running, combat flow, or sustained momentum.

8. Side tracking
   Camera moves parallel to the subject. Use for readable motion profile, travel, athletic rhythm, and continuous choreography.

9. Orbit
   Camera arcs around subject. Use for transformation, ritual, vertigo, showmanship, or emotional spiral.

10. Crane or boom
   Camera rises or descends. Use for release, downfall, mass reveal, or theatrical scale.

11. Pedestal
   Camera moves straight up or down without tilting. Use for controlled reveal or graphic vertical rhythm.

12. Handheld
   Use for urgency, instability, proximity, documentary pressure, exhaustion, or chaos.

13. Zoom
   Lens changes focal length. Use for stylized emphasis, unease, snap attention, or artificial music video energy.

14. Dolly zoom
   Camera moves while zoom compensates. Use for shock, reality bending, dread, or sudden internal rupture.

## Lens And Framing

1. Wide lens
   Emphasizes depth, speed toward camera, architecture, distortion, close physical energy.

2. Long lens
   Compresses space, makes pursuit feel tight, isolates subject, stacks background elements.

3. Fisheye
   Surreal bend, hyper kinetic body distortion, music video aggression, comic exaggeration.

4. Shallow focus
   Emotional isolation, intimate detail, audience attention locked to one plane.

5. Deep focus
   Choreography, multi plane staging, foreground and background interactions.

6. Negative space
   Loneliness, threat from emptiness, anticipation, ritual scale.

7. Silhouette
   Strong graphic read, anonymity, iconic action pose, theatrical lighting.

8. Frame within frame
   Doorways, windows, scaffolds, mirrors, beams, shadows. Use for entrapment or layered composition.

## Motion Semantics

Movement toward camera increases involvement or threat.

Movement away can imply escape, loss, retreat, or reveal.

Left to right often reads as forward journey in left to right reading cultures.

Diagonal upward motion suggests effort, ascent, conquest, or release.

Diagonal downward motion suggests collapse, panic, falling, or loss of control.

Vertical rise can suggest struggle or transcendence.

Sudden stillness after heavy motion can make the climax feel larger.

## Annotation System

Use annotation colors only when requested or useful:

1. Red arrows: body or object movement.
2. Blue arrows: camera movement.
3. Green marks: framing, crop, composition, negative space.
4. Orange marks: light direction, shadow, flare, practical source.
5. Purple marks: vocal, emotional, rhythm, music hit.
6. Black text: panel labels, lens notes, short action notes.

For monochrome storyboard drawings, keep the art black and white and reserve color for annotations.
